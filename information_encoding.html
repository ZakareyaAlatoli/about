The things we like

The Earth is a fairly big place. It's full of things most people will never encounter, but nevertheless might want to know about. For instance, deep in the jungles of southern Asia lives a peculiar animal called the siamang. You might be interested to know that they have an inflatable sack under their chins, and want to know what it looks like. Or you might want to know what sounds they make. You can learn this in seconds through a cursory search of "siamang" on YouTube.

But what if no recordings of the siamang existed? Not even a single photograph? Then how would you learn this information? Well, you could travel to their natural habitat to witness them firsthand, but that understandably wouldn't be worth the time and money for most.

I suppose we should be grateful someone else went through the effort of documenting these handsome creatures for us. Those adventurous folks were as captivated by them as you are now. But something you almost certainly did not consider, and something most of us take for granted, is how you can see simiangs without them actually being there.

A journey into abstraction

Obviously we can see things on our phones and computers from all around the world because of the internet, which just works, you know? But how exactly does it work? When you get right down to it, what is happening to move all this information around? To answer that, you need to put yourself into the shoes of a robot.

The way we learn about the world is through our senses: touch, smell, taste, sight, and hearing. They are the most basic way for us to know that there is something out there. If you were to break down the things that our senses...well...sense, how would you describe them? Each sense deals with a rudimentary object. For instance, sight deals with colors, hearing deals with sounds, touch with feelings, smell with scents, and taste with flavors.

When we think of an object looking a certain way, what we are really doing is imagining a batch of colors arranged in such a way that they represent a single thing. If you were to zoom in on a digital picture of say, your face, you would notice that the image is made up of small squares (called "pixels"), each with one solid color. But your brain doesn't see it that way. Your brain says, "This is my face", not "This is a grid of colored squares", even though both of those statements are correct.

What your brain is doing is called abstraction. It is taking some collection of things which are related in form or function, such as the pixels of an image, and treating it as a single entity. We make abstractions out of anything and everything. The word "universe" is an abstraction of all that exists packaged into a quaint, metaphorical box.
